Founding & Construction (12th - 14th Century)

Qutub Minar's tale starts with the Delhi Sultanate. Commissioned in **1192** by Qutb-ud-din Aibak, Delhi's first Sultan, the tower marked victory. Aibak built the foundation and first level of the "victory tower" before dying. His successor, **Shamsuddin Iltutmish**, added three more stories circa 1220 CE.

Over time, successive rulers shaped the form we see now. After the spire was struck by lightning in **1368**, Sultan **Firuz Shah Tughlaq** rebuilt the minaret, adding the **fifth story** to reach its final height.

The Qutb Complex: Beyond the Minar

Subsequent dynasties added to the Qutub complex. Key features include the ancient **Iron Pillar**, pre-dating the minar, a 4th-century marvel showcasing ancient Indian metalwork's resistance to rust. Additionally, the complex holds the incomplete **Alai Minar**, initiated by Alauddin Khalji around 1311.

Restoration and Recognition

In **1993**, the **Qutb Minar complex**, lauded for its significance, gained UNESCO World Heritage status.

Architecture & Design: Indo-Islamic Fusion

Style and Materials

The Qutub Minar is renowned for its **Indo-Islamic style**, blending Central Asian Islamic design with native art. Its **five separate levels** distinguish the minaret, each featuring a prominent balcony.

The initial three floors used **red sandstone**, contrasting with the later, Tughlaq-built levels, which featured **marble** and sandstone, lending a paler tone. The tower's base spanned approximately **14.3 meters**, narrowing to **2.7 meters** at its peak.

Decorative Features & Calligraphy

The outside boasts lavish ornamentation. The tower's façade is **ribbed**, sculpted with flowing curves and sharp angles, building a rising visual beat. Each level is graced with balconies held by **decorative corbels**, intricately sculpted with cellular or dripping-icicle designs (*muqarnas*).

The sandstone's surface displays Quranic verses in graceful **Arabic calligraphy**, alongside inscriptions in **Nagari characters**. This script mixture reflects the Sultanate's cultural fusion.

Quick FAQs

Is photography allowed at Qutub Minar?

Photography is permitted for personal use. Drone operation is strictly forbidden.

Can visitors climb to the top of Qutub Minar?

The tower's interior is off-limits to visitors, a closure enacted in 1981 due to safety concerns.
